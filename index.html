Context

We're at an inflection point in global development and AI – the next two years may have an outsized effect on the next century of impact.

The Current Landscape

  • Today's AI applications predominantly focus on the developed world. Access and applications in the developing world have been largely neglected - only a small set of actors are driving forward action, little funding has been allocated, and more nuanced pilots are often small-scale and bespoke.
  • AI models are rapidly progressing. Dario Amodei predicts AI models may surpass human capabilities "in almost everything" within 2-3 years, while McKinsey has reported its potential to unlock trillions of dollars in value.
  • Recent cuts of $60 billion in US foreign aid have created a significant unmet gap. USAID's closure is estimated to lead to 14 million deaths by 2030, alongside increases in infectious disease burdens, disability, severe malnutrition, pandemic risks, and lost human capital.

The Opportunity and Risk

Leveraging AI, low and middle-income countries could "leapfrog" traditional stages of development. Mobile money in Kenya demonstrates this power: M-Pesa leveraged high mobile phone penetration to provide a new model for banking access, increasing financial inclusion for tens of millions of people. Today, 80% of Kenyans use mobile money.

Conversely, without active support, developing economies may lag further. Government preparation is weak - South and Central Asia and sub-Saharan Africa recently ranked last in a government AI readiness index. Talent, compute access, high-quality datasets, and data centers are all concentrated in high-income countries.

Historical Precedents

History shows that without a clear driver and coordinated action, complex innovations in the developing world risk languishing or taking decades to drive uptake. However, there are successful examples:

  • GAVI's Advanced Market Commitments drove widespread adoption of the pneumococcal vaccine an estimated decade earlier than would have occurred otherwise.
  • The Green Revolution funded by Ford and Rockefeller Foundations drove dramatic reductions in poverty and infant mortality through introducing new farming technologies.

The Need

We urgently need a new model for launching AI-enabled, evidence-based development interventions – deployed at national scale.

  • Many non-profits and developing-world governments aren't resourced to fully capture this opportunity. While NGOs recognize AI's promise, many lack the technical resources to operationalize AI at national scale.
  • Some interventions will require new regulatory or policy frameworks to enable national-scale change in education and healthcare.
  • Meaningful, catalytic investment is needed. Today, over half of grants for AI and social impact efforts are less than $250,000, and many philanthropies don't yet account for AI in their strategic priorities.
  • We need a cross-sectoral team to ensure end-to-end efforts from upstream challenges to last-mile delivery become a reality.

Big Bets

To maximize the likelihood of this upside case - we must identify, prioritize, and act on big bets now.

We have evaluated approximately 30 high-potential interventions against their scale, evidence base, cost-effectiveness, tractability, and where AI is a key unlock. The highest priority interventions are moving forward in a rigorous vetting process.

Direct implementation is crucial to building in-depth knowledge on the barriers, opportunities, risks, and unknowns in AI in LMICs. This expertise will directly inform our broader ecosystem-building agenda.

We are operating against a two-year timeline to drive meaningful change. We aim to launch at least one at-scale intervention by the end of the two years.

Case Study: Delivering AI-enabled forecasts to smallholder farmers

There are over 475 million small-scale farmers globally. AI can generate more accurate, scalable forecasts across multiple variables and lead times. Delivering tailored forecasts can have transformative economic impacts:

  • Farmers in Benin and Colombia saw $100-350/year in financial benefit via SMS forecasts
  • Indian farmers gained approximately $334 million through improved seasonal rainfall forecasts
  • Forecasts can be delivered for pennies per farmer via SMS ($0.00019 in India, $0.03 in Ethiopia)

Theory of change: Reliable, timely, and accurate AI forecasts improve downstream decision-making by personalizing recommendations, improving trust, enabling two-way communication, and improving public decision making.

Other Potential Big Bets

AI in Education →

AI could shrink the gap between students in developed and developing economies. In India, targeted instruction yielded up to 4 additional learning-adjusted years of schooling for just $100/student. New AI-based solutions like Rori have shown improvements equivalent to a grade level in student learning outcomes.

Upstream Drug Development →

Creating financial incentives for investment, fast-tracking approvals, and coordinating delivery at scale for therapeutics addressing diseases of poverty - including tuberculosis, universal flu vaccines, Strep A, and congenital syphilis vaccines.

AI-Improved Medical Diagnosis →

The WHO predicts a shortfall of 11 million health workers by 2030. AI-supported medicine could enable high-quality diagnosis at remote clinics or even at home, transforming outcomes for hundreds of millions of people.

Our Vision

We're launching an organization to ensure AI's benefits reach many of the 3.5 billion people living in poverty globally.

  • Our approach will aim to jump progress curves forward in time. This work will be done at scale - looking to unlock access to AI innovations for a significant portion of people in the developing world in the coming decade.
  • There is not time to wait. We are first focusing on scaling one AI-enabled intervention nationally as a proof point of upside impact. In parallel, we will invest in broader ecosystem-building efforts.
  • This work has many unknowns - and we expect there may be major changes in scope. We believe it's crucial to have an ambitious north star while recognizing we'll learn and adapt as we go.

Cross-Sector Analysis: Executive Summary

We're at an inflection point in global development and AI – the next two years may have an outsized effect on the next century of impact.

Today's AI applications predominantly focus on advanced economies. Leveraging AI, low and middle-income countries could "leapfrog" traditional stages of development and drive massive efficiencies.

AI Opportunity Areas in LMICs

Downstream Agriculture →

The problem: 475 million small-scale farmers globally, 64% living on less than $1500 annually, face increasingly complex decisions amid climate variability with limited access to support.

AI opportunity: Help farmers increase crop yields, generating billions in income. AI improves weather forecasting accuracy and reduces costs compared to traditional extension programs.

Downstream Health →

The problem: Over half the global population lacks full coverage of essential health services; 60% of deaths in LMICs from treatable conditions occur due to poor quality care.

AI opportunity: Increase standards of physical and mental health treatment by improving diagnostics and democratizing access to care. High-quality health systems could prevent 2.5M cardiovascular deaths, 900K TB deaths, 1M newborn deaths, and 50% of maternal deaths annually.

Education →

The problem: 617M students fail to meet minimum proficiency levels in reading and math; 70% of 10-year-olds in LMICs were unable to understand a simple written text in 2022.

AI opportunity: Reduce educational attainment gaps by cost-effectively scaling evidence-backed tactics including personalized learning, structured lesson plans, and frequent feedback to millions of classrooms.

Humanitarian Response →

The problem: Approximately 30% of the global population lacks early-warning coverage for severe weather, leading to preventable loss of life and assets.

AI opportunity: Improve speed, accuracy, and scale of humanitarian response through better weather prediction, damage assessments, and infrastructure mapping.

Key Takeaways

Weather forecasting appears particularly impactful and tractable with potential to be delivered to tens of millions of farmers at extremely low marginal cost:

  • Impactful: AI models provide greater accuracy than traditional regional models, offer more precise forecasts, and unlock new types like seasonal predictions
  • Cost-Effective: Marginal costs are low (a few cents per SMS) with high expected benefit ratio at scale
  • Tractable: Pilots are already demonstrating early successes

We see high potential in layering digital farmer advisory services onto weather forecasting. Some are tractable near-term (using AI to synthesize agriculture best practices), while others require more investment (irrigation guidance based on soil sensors and satellite imagery).

Key Takeaways

The Problem: Over 4.5 billion people were not fully covered by essential health services as of 2021. There's an expected global shortfall of 10 million health workers by 2030, predominantly in LMICs.

High potential areas include:

  • AI for TB medical imaging: Approximately 10.6M people develop tuberculosis yearly, but approximately 3.1M cases go undiagnosed. Strong evidence supports Computer Aided Detection of chest X-rays for TB.
  • AI for mental health: Rules-constrained chatbots show potential to fill massive gaps in mental health services. Fully generative AI therapists remain too risky at this stage.
  • Models require clinical validation: Most results come from simulations or small pilots. More evidence needed on integration into LMIC clinical settings with limited digital infrastructure.

Key Takeaways

RCTs have validated AI education tools:

  • AI 1:1 tutoring in Ghana and Nigeria found improvements of 1-2 years of schooling after a few months
  • Brazil RCT found AI feedback increased essay scores across 19K students
  • These ideas face varying tractability levels, requiring widespread smartphone deployment and major changes to classroom operations

AI could scale evidence-backed solutions: Teaching at the Right Level and Structured Pedagogy programs are resource-intensive but highly effective. AI could scale these components to millions of classrooms at lower cost.

Key Takeaways

Major threats necessitate transformational R&D: By 2050, global food production needs to increase by 26-100%. Climate change threatens crop yields globally, especially for smallholder farmers.

Tractability barriers make this likely Low Readiness:

  • Most research is 5-15 years from commercialization
  • Structural barriers: GMO regulations, farmer trust/demand, IP licensing issues
  • Only 10% of SSA farmers access existing high-quality seeds

Coordinating player could: Fund research portfolio, coordinate IP licensing, establish advanced purchasing commitments, solve farmer uptake barriers, advocate for GMO-friendly regulations.

Global Health R&D

Traditional drug development requires 12-15 years and approximately $2.8 billion per approved drug, with only 7.9% clinical trial success rate. Novel therapies for neglected diseases are highly under-invested.

AI Transformation Across Value Chain

Discovery & Targeting Medium

AI analyzes biomedical datasets to understand disease biology and identify drug candidates. Drug repurposing holds particular promise. Limited by dataset quality and NTD disease complexity.

Preclinical Development Medium

AI improves in vitro testing accuracy. "Virtual patients" and Organs-on-chip systems have matched/surpassed some animal tests. FDA now encourages AI as animal testing replacement. Applications still nascent for complex diseases with limited data.

Clinical Testing Medium

AI can optimize patient recruitment, create synthetic control arms, improve protocol design. Most transformative applications face high data barriers in LMICs where datasets are limited.

Key Questions for Coordinated Action

  • Where is coordinated action to address data gaps most feasible (biobanks, clinical trials data, disease datasets)?
  • Should we temper expectations given NTD complexity (majority are parasitic)?
  • Which actors are best positioned for market-shaping investments?
  • What roles should coordinators play to prioritize diseases of poverty?

Early Warning Weather Forecasting - High Readiness

The Problem: About 30% of the global population lacks early-warning coverage for severe weather, leading to preventable loss of life and assets.

AI Benefits:

  • Improved accuracy and lead times (7 days advance flood warnings vs. 1-2 days traditional)
  • Analyzes wide range of data without highly skilled workforce
  • Reduces false alarms and builds trust in evacuation recommendations

Impact Potential: Investing $800M in early warning systems in LMICs would avoid losses of $3-16BN annually.

Critical Success Factors: Convincing users to trust forecasts, investing in quality training data for LMIC contexts, ensuring last-mile delivery reaches vulnerable populations.

Measurement & Evaluation

AI Intervention Description Readiness
Automated M&E systems Support NGOs/governments through entire M&E cycle Medium
Data collection networks Track key outcomes in high-poverty regions via AI voice agents Medium

Key Takeaways

Problem: Traditional evaluation in LMICs is expensive and slow, requiring specialists and large teams. Poverty data is often infrequent, outdated, or incomplete.

AI Opportunity: The end-to-end M&E process appears strong candidate for AI automation. Technology largely exists: LLMs for evaluation design, AI voice agents for data collection in local languages, AI tools for analysis and reporting.

Considerations: No clear market failure to address - private incentives might drive technology forward. Phone-based data collection using AI is largely untested in high-poverty regions.

Cross-Sector Hypotheses for Next Phase

Product Vision & Roadmap

  1. Think big but start small - stacking products over time: Identify biggest opportunity areas and build roadmaps that stack products, starting with near-term wins to drive early impact and adoption.
  2. Prioritize user adoption and behavior change: Leading indicators are more immediately measurable than multi-year RCTs. User adoption is key first step to long-term impact.
  3. Tradeoffs between breadth and depth: Consider tradeoffs between maximizing product reach versus improving outcomes for smaller groups.

Paths to Scale / Demand Drivers

  1. Governments are key path to national scale: Focus on tractability concerns and potential upsides to drive demand and scale.
  2. Free LLMs create competition: Alternative demand drivers (institutions, governments) may be more tractable, or partnerships with AI Labs to deploy on their platforms.

Adapting AI to LMIC Contexts

  1. Investments in tailoring: Gather high-quality datasets for local languages, weather observations, disease data. AI models are highly sensitive to training data quality.
  2. Benchmark models as they mature: LMIC implementers need clear ways to judge which models are accurate in what contexts.
  3. Multiple prerequisites needed: Hardware and internet access. Consider whether investments can unlock barriers at scale through mechanisms like advanced market commitments.

Anticipating Risks & Challenges

  1. Responsible AI considerations crucial: At intervention level (red teaming, rule-based systems, human oversight) and systems level (data privacy, algorithmic bias, job loss, security).
  2. Declining foreign aid environment: Need to drive efficiencies within systems as traditional funding decreases.
